70-minute accessible session

AI, Learning, and Productive Struggle

When generative AI can produce the work students used to do to learn, educators have to protect the thinking that still matters.

Micah J. Miner, CETL, Ed.S.Curriculum, Advocacy, and Policy
Open by naming the session as a learning design problem, not a tool demonstration. The chair's charge is to help doctoral students and alumni understand how generative AI can affect cognitive development and learning in positive and negative ways, and why educators must design for optimal human learning.
Opening case02 / 22

Two Students, Same Product, Different Learning

Student A

Uses AI to enter the task

Clarifies the prompt, asks for counterexamples, revises an argument, and can explain the choices.

Student B

Uses AI to exit the task

Generates a polished submission, changes a few words, and cannot explain the reasoning behind it.

Same artifact, different process02 / 22
This is the learning-first entry point. Both students may submit fluent work. The visible product is not enough to tell us whether learning occurred. Invite the audience to hold this distinction as the lens for the session.

Beyond risk/opportunity binaries03 / 22
The original qualifying-paper framing opened with media ecology. For this colloquium, keep theory downstream of the practical learning problem. Current school and university debates often organize around ban/embrace, cheat/innovate, and risk/opportunity. Those are real, but too small.
The real question04 / 22

What Happens When AI Can Produce the Work Students Used to Do to Learn?

The educational issue is not whether AI can make schoolwork easier. It is whether ease removes the cognitive work the assignment was designed to develop.

Core thesis04 / 22
State the thesis plainly: generative AI can support learning when it helps students enter, sustain, or deepen the work of thinking. It bypasses learning when it replaces the thinking the task was designed to develop.

Preserve the scholarly basis without making the room live inside it too early. Ong's core insight is that communication media restructure consciousness. Primary orality, literacy, and secondary orality are not merely technologies; each reorganizes memory, analysis, participation, and social presence. Cite Ong (1982/2002).
The analytical hinge07 / 22

Three Assumptions That No Longer Hold

Human origin

Symbolic content no longer has to begin in human consciousness.

Visible distribution

What reaches attention is sorted by opaque, personalized systems.

External media

The environment now reflects the learner's patterns back to them.

Why Ong needs extension07 / 22
This compresses the qualifying-paper slide on three assumptions. Across Ong's stages, humans originated symbolic content, distribution was more visible, and media were encountered as external environments. Generative and personalized systems disrupt all three.
First extension08 / 22

Algorithmic Secondary Orality

Human-created, algorithmically distributed.

human createsfeed sortsattention changes

After the feed replaces the shared schedule, two people open the same platform and enter different symbolic worlds.

Human authorship remains; distribution changes08 / 22
Use Facebook News Feed in 2006 as the concrete example from the source deck. The point is not Facebook itself, but the shift from human-created broadcast schedules to computationally sorted, personalized streams optimized for engagement. The source content remains human-authored.
Second extension09 / 22
Tertiary algorithmicity

A media environment in which algorithmic systems both curate and generate symbolic content, making human authorship optional at scale.

Algorithmically created and distributed09 / 22
Preserve the exact required definition. Explain why the term is algorithmicity rather than orality: the oral-literate continuum no longer captures the primary transformation. Whether output is spoken or written matters less than whether a human or an algorithm produced it. Mention prior tertiary orality extensions: Mayer (2009), Turner and Allen (2013), Angel-Botero and Alvarado-Duque (2016), Soffer (2020), Heyd (2021), Ryu (2023), and Cordon-Garcia and Munoz-Rico (2024).

Noetic, rhetorical, existential risks10 / 22
This adapts the source deck's noetic displacement, rhetorical saturation, and existential abstraction into accessible language. These are not moral accusations against students. They are descriptions of what a media environment makes easy.
Educational stakes11 / 22

The Educational Stakes: Not Just Integrity

Academic integrity asks, "Who produced this?" Learning design asks, "What thinking did the student have to do?"

Integrity matters, but it is not enough11 / 22
Connect back to the two-student opening. Integrity policies can tell us whether a rule was followed, but they often cannot tell us whether cognition developed. This is where the learning science foundation enters.
Kapur's learning science lens12 / 22
Unproductive success

Correct-looking academic performance without the cognitive struggle required for durable understanding.

Unproductive success

Correct output, weak understanding.

Productive success

Correct output, understanding aligned.

Unproductive failure

Wrong output, little growth.

Productive failure

Struggle that prepares future learning.

After Kapur, 201612 / 22
Preserve the required definition and the Kapur citation. Generative AI can reduce extraneous load, such as formatting or mechanics. The concern is when it also removes germane load: the schema construction that is the learning. The artifact is present; the developmental process may not be.
Learning science foundation13 / 22

Why Struggle Matters for Learning

Retrieve: bring knowledge back without simply looking it up.
Generate: produce an idea before seeing the answer.
Revise: compare, explain, and improve thinking over time.
Productive struggle, desirable difficulties, germane load13 / 22
Keep this accessible. The point is not that all struggle is good. The point is that durable learning often depends on retrieval, generation, explanation, revision, feedback, and transfer. This connects to productive failure, desirable difficulties, and cognitive load theory without overloading the slide.
Response framework14 / 22
Pedagogical friction

The intentional design of useful difficulty so students still do the thinking, explaining, revising, and meaning-making that learning requires.

Useful difficulty by design14 / 22
Preserve the required definition. Emphasize that this is not a call to make school harder, ban tools, or romanticize struggle. It is a framework for distinguishing the difficulty that builds capacity from the difficulty that merely blocks access.
The framework15 / 22

Four Forms of Friction: Head, Room, World, System

Head

Noetic friction
Thinking work.

Room

Rhetorical friction
Dialogue work.

World

Existential friction
Ownership, presence, and embodied accountability.

System

Infrastructural friction
Systems that protect learning.

Bypass, simulation, abstraction, conditions15 / 22
Preserve the four required dimensions. Noetic friction is internal cognitive work: comprehending, synthesizing, arguing, revising. Rhetorical friction is the work of dialogue with real others. Existential friction is ownership and accountability. Infrastructural friction is policy, curriculum, assessment, professional development, and system design that makes the other three possible.
The equity test16 / 22

Productive vs. Exclusionary Friction

Productive friction

  • Builds capacity
  • Prepares future learning
  • Protects thinking

Exclusionary friction

  • Blocks access
  • Measures the wrong thing
  • Preserves inequity

The same AI tool can reduce both. That is why professional judgment matters.

Useful difficulty is not the same as gatekeeping16 / 22
This preserves the source deck's equity guardrail. An argument for preserving friction can become an argument for preserving inequity unless educators ask whose learning the difficulty supports and whose participation it obstructs. Examples: inaccessible formats and language barriers unrelated to the learning goal are exclusionary; critique, revision, and argument construction can be productive.
Decision tool17 / 22

AI Use Decision Tool: Reduce Barriers, Protect Thinking

Use AI to reduce barriers when...

  • The barrier is not the learning objective.
  • The student remains responsible for meaning.
  • The tool improves access to the task.

Limit AI when...

  • The tool performs the target thinking.
  • The process becomes invisible.
  • The student cannot explain or defend the work.
Reduce barriers, protect thinking17 / 22
This is the practical hinge for the room. The decision is not "AI or no AI." It is: What is the task designed to develop? What friction is productive? What friction is exclusionary? What evidence will show that the student still did the learning work?

Implications19 / 22

Curriculum, Advocacy, Policy

Curriculum

Make thinking visible before, during, and after AI use.

Advocacy

Name productive friction as a protected educational value.

Policy

Close the learning-science gap in AI guidance.

From individual choices to institutional design19 / 22
Preserve the source deck's three implication columns. Curriculum: process-oriented assessment, sequencing, oral components, peer critique, reflection. Advocacy: counter the efficiency-personalization-scalability discourse. Policy: most district and institutional AI policy addresses integrity, privacy, and bias; it often says less about what learning requires.

This retains the dissertation bridge from the source deck. Frame the dissertation as an empirical next step: educators are already making situated judgments, but institutions need better language and evidence for when friction is productive, exclusionary, or both.
